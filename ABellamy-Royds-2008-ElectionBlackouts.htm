BLACKOUT – 70 years of arguments over a uniquely Canadian infringement of Freedom of the Press

Amelia Bellamy-Royds, 25 November 2008

A tale of two elections

November 4, 2008:  Election Day in the United States of America.  Across the country, voters wake up to the news that – with two polls reporting – Democrat Barack Obama is leading Republican John McCain in the presidential race by 16 votes.

The results came from the small towns of Dixville Notch and Hart’s Location, New Hampshire.  Under New Hampshire law, a polling district may close down, count the ballots, and report the results once all eligible voters have voted.  The 21 voters in Dixville Notch and 29 in Hart’s Location gathered at midnight to be the first in the country to exercise their democratic franchise.

This quirky tradition is a favourite of national and international news organizations, making residents of these tiny towns media stars for a few hours.  The results may be insignificant in the final nationwide tally, but they provide colour to morning newscasts on election day. 

Three weeks earlier, October 14, 2008:  Voters in Canada head to the polls in our own federal election.  There are no midnight tallies in small towns, but some votes are nonetheless finished sooner than others.  Staggered voting hours may reduce the effect of Canada’s six time zones, but polls still close in Newfoundland and Labrador three hours before they do in British Columbia.

This means that fairly reliable counts are available in Atlantic Canada at a time when afternoon radio hosts in Vancouver are still reminding their listeners to stop by the polling station on their way home.  But B.C. broadcasters can’t use the incoming results from Happy Valley-Goose Bay or Bonavista to add some colour to their commentary.  Doing so would invite a $25,000 fine.

Since 1938, it has been illegal to publish or broadcast election night results from one part of Canada in another part of the country where the polls are still open. 

The pros and cons of the blackout have been debated by parliamentary committees and Royal Commissions.  It has been challenged all the way to the Supreme Court.  It causes headaches for national newsmedia, and has never been fully enforceable. 

It seems a particular affront in today’s age of instant information.  In the Montreal Gazette the morning after the election, columnist Mike Boone whined “Is there anything more annoying than a news blackout in the Information Age?”[1]  But the arguments for and against the blackout have changed remarkably little in the 70 years it has been in effect.

The problem with instant news

It may seem like the age of bloggers and satellite television is vastly different from the era of telegraph transmissions and extra editions, but in 1938 politicians had already felt the sting of news that travelled faster than the Earth could turn.

In federal elections in 1930 and 1935, newspapers in western Canada had published special editions during the afternoon of election day, announcing the results from the East.[2]  Papers were handed to electors as they headed out to vote, and radio stations likewise carried the news.  In at least one case, the reports were grossly inaccurate, presumably to try to influence voters to side with the alleged “winning” party. 

In 1938, Parliament was debating a new Dominion Elections Act, and members of all parties agreed that something should be done to limit the impact of these early news reports. [3]  Tommy Douglas, then an MP for the Co-operative Commonwealth Federation, summed up the extent of the problem:

Right across the street from where people were voting in my constituency [during the last election] was a huge sign on which were the words, “Liberal landslide in the east.”  It might just as well be a Conservative of a C.C.F. in the next election.  It is bound to have a psychological effect on the voter who is going to cast his ballot.

The Conservative MP from Vancouver South complained that it “was exasperating that our electors were not being given an opportunity to cast their ballots without knowing the results in the east.” 

Three choices

The committee that studied the bill had proposed one solution:  stagger the voting hours, so that polls in the Maritimes closed an hour later (at 7 p.m.) than most of the country, while polls in the West closed an hour earlier (at 5 p.m.).

Politicians from both sides of the country denounced the plan.  Those in Nova Scotia were worried about citizens voting in the dark.  Those from British Columbia were worried about voters unable to get home from work in time to vote.

Some MPs suggested that the voting hours should be left alone, but the ballots not counted until polls had closed nationwide.  Charles Power, the Liberal Minister of Pensions, knocked down that proposal with the suggestion that the “idle hands” of election staff “will find evil to do.”  Others argued that it would keep elections officials working too late and the public waiting around impatiently.

The third option, suggested by a number of members, was to simply ban the transmission or publication of results.  The main objections raised to this proposal were that it would be ineffective, since radio broadcasts could be made through the United States, and that it contradicted freedom of the press.  Minister Power argued that “a greater amount of abuse than that now existing would be necessary to warrant the parliament of Canada preventing newspapers from publishing accurate news.”

Nonetheless, with no support for the first two proposals, the blackout was adopted, making it illegal to publish election results in any area where the polls had not closed.

Reforming Electoral Democracy: the Lortie Commission

Fifty years later, much had changed in the way Canadians communicated with one another, but much had stayed the same in Canadian electoral laws. 

In an April 1989 speech from the Throne, the newly re-elected Mulroney government promised a Royal Commission on electoral reform.  Businessman Pierre Lortie, previously the chair of Quebec grocery chain Provigo and later the head of Bombardier, was selected as chair when the commission was established in November of that year.[4] 

The commission held hearings across the country in 1990 and 1991.  Proposed changes ranged from the grand to the administrative.  Ban political donations from corporations and unions.  Limit advertising on election issues by anyone other than candidates and parties.  Create a permanent voters’ list to replace the enumeration process.  Set aside seats for aboriginal Canadians.  Allow judges and prisoners to vote.  Abolish a law prohibiting the sale of alcohol on during voting hours.  Forbid the publication of opinion polls during a campaign.  Legally mandate televised debates by party leaders.  And, of course, do something about the problem of elections being decided before Western Canadians had voted.

At a commission hearing in the Yukon, the territory’s Senator, Paul Lucier, argued “Western and northern Canadians going to the polls when the election results have already been broadcast is an insult that can no longer be tolerated.”[5]  Lucier suggested that polls in Western time zones could open the evening before election day in order to maintain ease of access. 

Even before the Commission began hearings, commission chair Lortie indicated that the members were considering a proposal to stagger the voting hours across the country so that polls would open and close simultaneously, regardless of time zone.[6]  Under this system, polls would be open from noon to 10 p.m. in Newfoundland and from 8:30 a.m. to 5:30 p.m. in B.C. and the Yukon.  There would be no need for a broadcasting blackout.

The arguments against the staggered voting hours were the same as they had been in 1938:  it would keep election workers up too late in the East and interfere with the business day in the West.  Lortie suggested that the second half of that problem could be solved by holding elections on Sundays.[7]

The Royal Commission on Electoral Reform and Party Financing released its final report on February 13, 1992.[8]  On the subject of controlling the impact of eastern Canadian election results on western Canadian voters, commission members examined the same three options that had been discussed in Parliament in 1938. 

On the blackout law, the report argued it “had been rendered obsolete” by new technologies, and pointed out (just as it had been in 1938) that Western Canadians could learn election results from broadcasters in U.S. border cities. 

On the option of delaying the counting of votes, it concluded that “if staff had to stay for this additional time, it would appear logical to keep the polls open.”

Instead, the commissioners chose the option that had been dismissed five decades earlier, staggering the voting hours.  The report recommended that polls in Ontario and East be open from 9:30 a.m. to 9:30 p.m., local time, and that voting hours in Central and Mountain time zones be set to so to be simultaneous with those in Ontario and Quebec.  Hours in the Pacific time zone would be a half-hour delayed, from 7 a.m. to 7 p.m. local time.  Lortie’s previous suggestion of Sunday elections was not endorsed.

Although the proposed system would have polls closing in Atlantic Canada up to two hours before close in the West, the Commission recommended the abolition of the blackout law.  The report concluded that the release of results from this very limited number of seats “would not constitute a major problem so long as other results from eastern Canada were not available.”

A Vancouver Sun editorial the next day called the proposal “the queerest (version) yet” of staggered voting hours, saying “it would confuse everybody without solving the problem.”[9]

New law, old arguments

As with many Royal Commissions, the recommendations were not immediately adopted by the government.  The 1993 election was campaigned under the old rules, and the new Liberal government was not in a rush to implement the proposals.

Changes to voting hours were one of the first amendments enacted.  The necessary amendments were added during committee study of a bill establishing the permanent voters’ list.  The changes were inspired in part by a private member’s bill introduced earlier in the session by Vancouver East MP Anna Terrana.  Terrana’s bill advocated simultaneous voting, but the amendments adopted by the committee were closer to the Lortie Commission’s proposal.  The only difference was that polls in Atlantic Canada would close at 8:30 p.m. local time, not 9:30, making the maximum difference in poll closures from East to West three hours instead of two.

Even with this minor difference, the only results available before polls closed in the West would be those from Atlantic Canada.

The debate on the changes brought up the same three options: staggered voting hours, delayed vote counts, or the blackout.  Stephen Harper proposed an amendment which would include all three elements, just to be sure.[10]

In the end, the staggered voting hours were adopted, but the prohibition on reporting early results was left untouched.  A major overhaul of the Canada Elections Act in 2000 also preserved the then-62-year-old restriction.

In September 2000 the blackout law earned some notoriety when it was applied to two by-elections held on opposite sides of the country.  Because of the law, results from Kings-Hants, Nova Scotia could not be broadcast nationally or published online until polls closed in Okanagan–Coquihalla, B.C., four hours later.  A Nova Scotia man who contravened the blackout by posting results on his website later had his house raided and his computer seized by Elections Canada officials.[11]

ElectionResultsCanada.com

Paul Bryan of Burnaby, B.C., thought this was unreasonable.  In preparation for the November 27, 2000 federal election, he set up a web site, ElectionResultsCanada.com, to publish Atlantic Canada election results during the blackout. 

In a press release issued one month before the election, Bryan indicated that he was intentionally challenging a “gag law” he believed was contrary to the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms.  “Election gag laws have no place in a free and democratic society,” the release quoted him as saying.  “Ordinary citizens must step up and challenge such laws or else face losing their rights altogether.”[12]

The press release garnered Bryan attention from news media across the country.  It also earned him the attention of Elections Canada.  A week before the election, he received an email from legal counsel to the Commissioner of Canada Elections.  It referenced the media coverage, informed him of the legal prohibition and penalties, and concluded:  “We trust that, as you are now aware of the prohibition, you will comply with the Canada Elections Act by refraining to publish the results until all polls across Canada have closed on election day.” [13]  Undaunted, Bryan posted the letter on his website.

On election night, ElectionResultsCanada.com carried early results from Atlantic Canada, taken from satellite television.  Within weeks of the election, Bryan was subject to a search warrant and then charged with violating the Canada Elections Act. 

Reasonable limits

The case reached court in early 2003.  Bryan applied to the trial judge, Judge K. A. P. D. Smith of the B.C. Provincial Court, to have the pertinent sections of the Canada Elections Act struck down as an unconstitutional infringement of his right to freedom of expression.

Canadian judges use a two-step process to determine whether a law contravenes the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms.  First, the applicant must demonstrate that the law infringes a right guaranteed by the Charter.  If the court agrees that it does, the government prosecution has to defend the law on the basis of section 1 of the Charter:

The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms guarantees the rights and freedoms set out in it subject only to such reasonable limits prescribed by law as can be demonstrably justified in a free and democratic society.

In assessing whether the limits are reasonable, the court considers not only the purpose of the limit, but also whether the benefit is greater than the harm.

In the case against Paul Bryan, the trial judge and all subsequent appeal judges agreed that the election night blackout infringes Canadians’ guaranteed “freedom of thought, belief, opinion and expression, including freedom of the press and other media of communication.”[14]  The debate was whether the infringement could be “demonstrably justified in a free and democratic society.”

Judge Smith ruled that the limit was reasonable and the law constitutional.  With his constitutional challenge dismissed, Bryan did not dispute the evidence of the case.  He was convicted of the charge and fined $1000.

Bryan and his lawyers appealed; their arguments were heard by Justice S. Kelleher of the B.C. Supreme Court in September 2003. 

In re-examining the case, Justice Kelleher focused on the Lortie commission’s recommendation to drop the blackout law.  He concluded that there was no “pressing and substantial” need for a blackout, since results from Atlantic Canada alone could not decide an election.  

Justice Kelleher released his decision on October 23, 2003, overturning the conviction and striking down the election night blackout.

A short-lived victory

The government appealed Justice Kelleher’s decision to the B.C. Court of Appeal.  Leave to appeal was granted in March 2004, but a hearing was not held until the next May.

In the meantime, a federal election was called for June 28, 2004.  Justice Kelleher’s decision meant that the blackout law could not be enforced in British Columbia, but his decision was not binding on the rest of the country.  Elections Canada, however, announced that, to “achieve uniform application of the Act across the country,” they would not enforce the blackout, anywhere.[15]

In the 2004 election, therefore, national newsmedia and personal websites were free to publish all election results as they were announced, coast to coast.  Elections Canada’s own website, however, did not post results until polls had closed nationwide.[16] 

The blackout had been lifted, but only temporarily.  On 20 May 2005, the British Columbia Court of Appeal, in a 2 to 1 judgement, re-affirmed the constitutionality of the blackout law and restored Paul Bryan’s conviction.

Bryan appealed to the Supreme Court of Canada, which heard arguments on October 16, 2006.   

Up until this point, the only evidence that had been considered was the Lortie commission report and the testimony of Robert MacDermid, a political science professor from York University.  MacDermid had testified, based on studies of United States presidential elections, that voter turnout in Western time zones dropped when media reports announced a winner before the polls had closed. 

In the Supreme Court, the government introduced the results of a poll[17] on the blackout law, conducted during the 2005/2006 election campaign.  The poll concluded that 70% of Canadians “thought people should not be able to know election results from other provinces before their polls close.”  On the opposite side of the debate, the Court heard arguments from a coalition of national broadcasters, who testified about the technical difficulties of implementing the blackout.

Protecting “informational equality”

On March 15, 2007, a majority of the Supreme Court of Canada ruled that election results blackout was constitutional.[18]  In the words of Justice Michel Bastarache, “the salutary effects of s. 329 outweigh the deleterious effects.”  In layman’s terms, the pros outweigh the cons.

According to Bastarache and the other justices who upheld the blackout, the key value protected by the law is “informational equality.”  Because Newfoundlanders cannot know how other parts of the country have voted before they cast their ballots, neither should British Columbians. 

The justices did not put considerable weight on the possibility that election outcomes could be changed by publishing early results, but they did consider the importance of maintaining the “subjective perceptions of Canadian voters that the electoral system is fair” in light of the polling results.

The court’s decision was far from unanimous:  four out of nine justices disagreed with the ruling.  The dissenting justices argued that within the context of staggered voting hours, the blackout had little effect on the outcome or perceived fairness of the election.  While the five justices who upheld the blackout were of the opinion that a three-hour blackout was only a minor delay, the dissenters argued that “News is news precisely because of its immediacy” and that Canadians have a right to learn the outcome of an election as soon as possible.

Current and Future Complications

The decision of the Supreme Court did not address all possible legal questions arising from the blackout law.  One which remains is where to draw the line between private and public communications.

The current wording of the blackout provision prohibits the “transmission” of results (or purported results) to “the public” in a blacked-out electoral district.[19]  Posting material on a public website, as Paul Bryan did, has been clearly accepted as transmission of information to the public.  But what if the information is posted to a website with very limited access, such as a facebook page?  How would sending the results to one’s online “friends” differ from picking up a phone and calling family on the other side of the country?

On a more practical level, there is the question of whether the law can ever be enforced given the diversity of ways in which Canadians can now communicate with one another.  On Twitter networks on election night – whereby individuals can broadcast news and gossip by text message (“Tweets”) to anyone who is listening for certain key words – results flowed freely while the blackout was still in effect. 

Some participants were cautious, asking for example, “s**t, are we even allowed to tweet results if polls still open in BC?”[20]  Others encouraged defiance of the law:  “Come on twitter, let's make history and break the news blackout,” urged one user.[21]  Six weeks after the election, there have been no reports of “Tweeters” have had their computers seized by Elections Canada officials wielding search warrants. 

Will the blackout ever be lifted?

The Supreme Court may have ruled that the election night blackout law is constitutional, but the law can still be changed by Parliament.  If public sentiment is strong, politicians tend to follow suit.

Nonetheless, Canada’s geography hasn’t changed, so the same options – and arguments – remain. 

If the blackout is dropped but current voting hours maintained, Atlantic Canadian results will be available for late voters in the West, and could potentially impact election outcomes. 

If voting hours were changed to be completely simultaneous, voters and election staff at both ends of the country would be inconvenienced by too late or too early voting times.

And if vote counts were delayed, everyone would complain:  election workers would have to stay up late out east, and news junkies would have to wait just as long or longer for their results.

But then again, if the residents of Dixville Notch can all show up to vote at midnight, what are Canadians complaining about?
